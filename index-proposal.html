Why simulation

Spreadsheets lie about queues.

Queueing formulas assume neat averages and steady-state behaviour. Real systems are messier than that. Variability compounds, routing creates unequal experiences, and shared resources produce knock-on effects that a spreadsheet simply cannot reveal.

Variability is non-linear

A server at 90% utilisation is not a slightly worse version of one at 50%. Waiting times accelerate, often dramatically, and simple intuition breaks down.

Distributions matter, not just averages

Service times with the same mean can create very different queue behaviour. Averages hide uncertainty. Simulation exposes it.

Systems interact in ways formulas cannot capture

Priority rules, shared staff, blocking, and starvation produce effects that only emerge when you model the system event by event.

Worked example

A&E triage, modelled in 20 minutes.

This should be the centrepiece of the page. It shows what simulation is for, why the product matters, and where AI interpretation helps the most. It moves the product from abstract promise into a concrete decision-support tool.

Scenario

An emergency department receives patients at an average rate of 12 per hour. High-acuity patients route to a priority queue. Low-acuity patients join a secondary queue. Three clinicians are shared across both streams.

An Erlang-C approximation can produce one average wait estimate, but it cannot tell you how that wait splits between patient groups or what happens to uncertainty as the system approaches capacity.

After 30 replications with warm-up removal, the model reveals a large gap in experience between patient groups and points directly to the structural trade-off driving that result.

AI insight

Low-acuity wait variance is materially high, which means this is not just a fairness issue but a predictability issue. Explore dedicated low-acuity capacity or redesigned routing before committing to current staffing assumptions.
